# Chapter 13: Gamemastering

## Getting Started

### Scheduling
Set expectations for: number of sessions, frequency, and average session length.

### Theme and Safety
Before play begins, discuss tone, content, and comfort levels (see **Safety Guide** at end of this chapter).

### Setting Knowledge
Gauge how familiar players are with the Stormlight Archive. Adjust how you present lore.

### Character Creation Guidance
Let players know their starting level and any story constraints. If a player is unhappy with their character mid-campaign, consider allowing a **Self-Reflection** rebuild (see Chapter 9).

---

## Calling for Skill Tests

Ask yourself before rolling:
1. Is there a realistic chance of both success **and** failure?
2. Will the outcome **change the story**?
3. Could the test introduce interesting **ramifications**?

If the answer to any is yes, call for a test. If the task is trivially easy or certainly impossible, narrate the result directly.

---

## Using the Plot Die

**When to raise the stakes:**
- About **1/3 of tests** (roughly)
- Tests that directly advance a character's goal, purpose, or obstacle
- Tense, high-stakes, or dramatically important moments
- When you want to introduce uncertainty for the fun of it

**When NOT to raise the stakes:**
- Routine preparation tasks
- Activities with no real tension

### Plot Die in Different Scene Types

**Outside Combat:** Use the plot die during:
- Significant deception or social manipulation
- Key stealth or infiltration tests
- Tests against hostile informants or dangerous NPCs

**In Combat:** Use sparingly — even small results can swing fights. Good moments include:
- A PC just dropped to 0 health and rallies
- A one-on-one duel against a significant villain
- Combat tied directly to a character's goal or obstacle

### Collaborative Plot Die Use
Open the floor for players to **suggest Opportunity and Complication outcomes**. Players can suggest how their rolled ○ is spent; you decide how to spend C.

---

## Using Opportunities and Complications

Four creative lenses:

1. **Another Axis** — Opportunities/Complications represent a secondary outcome (speed, collateral, hidden info)
2. **Alternate Skills** — What other skills could come into play here?
3. **Dumb Luck** — Random incidental events unrelated to the skill being tested
4. **Advancing the Story** — Tie planned plot beats to rolled results

---

## Structuring a Game Session

Chain scenes together organically:
- Combats, conversations, endeavors all flow into each other
- Rest and downtime punctuate the action
- Generally: several **easy** combats, a couple **average** ones, and one **hard** combat between long rests

---

## Using Adversaries

Each adversary has a **profile** (background + tactics) and a **stat block** (simplified rules). Stat blocks include:

- **Tier** and **Role** (Minion / Rival / Boss)
- Attributes and defenses
- Health (with adjustable range)
- Focus and Investiture
- Deflect value
- Movement rate and senses
- Skills (only those with ranks)
- Features (always-active abilities)
- Actions (attack and special actions)
- Opportunities and Complications specific to that adversary

### Adversary Roles

| Role | Health | Threat | Special Rule |
|------|--------|--------|-------------|
| **Minion** | Low | 0.5 | Can't critically hit; immediately defeated when suffering an injury |
| **Rival** | Normal | 1 | No special role rules |
| **Boss** | High | 4 | Takes a fast AND slow turn each round; can spend 1 focus for extra ▶ or to remove a condition |

### Defeated Adversaries
- Minor NPCs defeated at 0 health (PC chooses: dead or Unconscious)
- Major NPCs can make injury rolls like PCs at GM discretion

## Building Combat Scenes

### Threat Values

| Party Tier vs. Adversary Tier | Minion | Rival | Boss |
|-------------------------------|--------|-------|------|
| 3 tiers lower | 4 | 8 | 32 |
| 2 tiers lower | 2 | 4 | 16 |
| 1 tier lower | 1 | 2 | 8 |
| **Same tier** | **0.5** | **1** | **4** |
| 1 tier higher | 0.25 | 0.5 | 2 |
| 2 tiers higher | 0 | 0.25 | 1 |
| 3 tiers higher | 0 | 0 | 0.5 |

### Combat Difficulty
- **Easy:** Total threat = ½ number of PCs
- **Average:** Total threat = number of PCs
- **Hard:** Total threat = 1.5× number of PCs

### Tips for Varied Combats
- **Vary terrain:** Elevation, distance, hazards, difficult terrain
- **Consider the previous scene:** Low focus from a negotiation, no gear from an ambush
- **Add non-combat objectives:** Race to a gemheart, protect a hostage, capture don't kill
- **Offer resources:** Infused spheres, volatile materials, precariously placed boulders
- **Mix enemies:** Use adversaries who synergize with each other

### Challenging Radiants
Tools that counter Surgebinding:
- **White sand** — detects Investiture use
- **Secretspren** — scream toward Investiture sources
- **Raysium weapons** — drain Stormlight on hit/graze
- **Larkin** — feed on Stormlight directly
- **Anti-Stormlight** — combustive reaction with Stormlight; lethal
- **The Weeping** — four-week period with no highstorms (no Stormlight)

## Mass Warfare

Battles are a **setting** for multiple scenes — not a single combat. Structure them as **phases** with objectives, complications, and evolving outcomes.

### Defining Scope

**Number of Troops:**
| Scale | Tier |
|-------|------|
| Hundreds | Even tier 1 PCs can have a large impact |
| Thousands | Tier 2+ characters play pivotal roles |
| Tens of thousands | Tier 3+ (Shardbearers, Radiants) needed |

**Time:**
- Minutes / Hours / Days — determines session pacing and rest opportunities

**Party Role:**
- Caught in the Fray — survival-focused, one small objective
- Lynchpin Soldiers — key missions affecting the battle's outcome
- Commanders — strategy + direct combat

### Battle Phases
Each phase = one side pursuing an objective. Map phases in advance with success and failure branches.

### In the Fray
Represent surrounding chaos narratively:
- **Opportunities/Complications** — use plot die results to show the battle shifting
- **Events** — count down to arrival of reinforcements, a front pushing forward, etc.
- **Tests** — Leadership to rally soldiers, Intimidation to push back a front

## Structuring a Campaign

### Awarding Levels
- Keep all PCs at the **same level** unless the story demands otherwise
- Award levels at **meaningful story milestones**
- Plan tier pacing: How many sessions per tier? What's appropriate for each?

### Awarding Goals/Rewards
- **Goal advancement:** ~once per session (twice for exceptional moments)
- **Goal conclusion:** Work collaboratively; the reward should be meaningful
- The GM can grant spontaneous rewards when the story warrants

### Starting at Higher Levels
Use the **Higher-Level Starting Rewards** table:

| Starting Tier | Marks | T1 | T2 | T3 | T4+ |
|--------------|-------|----|----|----|----|
| Tier 1 (lvl 2–5) | 200 mk | 1 | — | — | — |
| Tier 2 (lvl 6–10) | 1,000 mk | 2 | 1 | — | — |
| Tier 3 (lvl 11–15) | 2,000 mk | 2 | 1 | 1 | — |
| Tier 4+ (lvl 16+) | 5,000 mk | 2 | 1 | 1 | 1 |

> Swearing a Radiant Ideal counts as a reward — plan accordingly when placing higher-tier Radiant talents.

## Safety Guide

### Core Principle
Safety centers around **comfort, acknowledgment, and shared space**. Safety tools aren't a limitation on storytelling — they're how everyone can trust each other enough to explore difficult themes.

### Session Zero
Before your first session, discuss:
- **Campaign expectations** (setting, tone, theme, homebrew rules)
- **Table expectations** (scheduling, language, cell phone use)
- **Safety tools** (lines and veils, check-in procedures)
- **Character creation**

### Lines and Veils
- **Lines** — topics that are completely off the table (never referenced even off-camera)
- **Veils** — topics that can exist off-camera but aren't depicted in detail

### Checking In
During play, watch for discomfort. If needed:
- "I'm so sorry, I forgot. Let's backtrack and try this a different way."
- Ask openly: "How can we approach this instead?"

### Topics to Consider

| Topic | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| Slavery | Central to Roshar's history; singers experienced horrific oppression. Handle with care and space. |
| Mental illness | Never gamify for entertainment. Research before including; ask for group consent. |
| Speciesism/Singers | Afford singers the same empathy as humans in real-world discussion. |
| Eye shade discrimination | Avoid real-world racial coding when depicting lighteyes/darkeyes. |
| Gender roles | Vorinism imposes strict roles; discuss how much your group wants to engage with this. |
| Gender identity | Stormlight can heal bodies to match spiritual identity; non-conforming characters shouldn't face persecution without group consent. |
| Casteism/Classism | Widespread on Roshar; decide what level to include. |
| Sexuality | Many cultures only recognize heterosexual norms; adjust as needed for your group. |
| Disability | Players of disabled characters control their own narrative. No required mechanical impairment. |
| Abuse | Discuss limits on depictions involving vulnerable groups. |
| Gore/Horror | Agree on a rating-style threshold before play. |
| Intimacy | Requires advanced notice; frequent check-ins; err heavily toward "fade to black." |

### Including Marginalization Responsibly

If the group agrees to include prejudice affecting PCs:
1. **Seek Consent** before directing prejudice at a specific player's character
2. **Shift the Point of View** to the marginalized character — center their experience, not the perpetrator's
3. **Support Each Other** — voice support for the targeted character after the scene

**Caution:** Interparty marginalization based on real-world identities requires unanimous agreement and careful handling.
